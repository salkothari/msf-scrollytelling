The Problem · 1 of 2

Biologically hard
to detect

1
Lower bacterial loads
  • Children have lower bacterial loads than adults
  • Standard TB diagnostic tests designed for adults frequently return false negatives
  • Children with TB go undiagnosed and untreated
2
Can't produce sputum
  • Most diagnostics rely on sputum samples
  • Most children under 5 can't cough up sputum on demand
  • Primary specimen collection method unusable for children
3
Non-specific symptoms
  • Fever, fatigue, weight loss — TB symptoms in children overlap with dozens of common illnesses
  • This causes misdiagnosis and dangerous delays
4
Extrapulmonary TB
  • Children get TB in lymph nodes, bones, brain and other non-lung sites more frequently than adults
  • Sample collection and bacteriological confirmation becomes harder

Public health systems and biotechnology prioritise what is scalable, profitable and measurable, resulting in a self-reinforcing cycle that keeps children with TB sidelined.

The Solution

Treatment Decision Algorithms:
a diagnostic flowchart that saves lives

Identifies TB in children under 10 without advanced technology or specialists
Systematises non-specific symptoms, TB exposure, and basic test results into a cohesive scoring system
Endorsed by the World Health Organization in 2022
Score > 10 triggers TB treatment — no lab confirmation required
Two versions: A for clinics with chest X-ray, B for those without — identical logic, different scoring weights
Based on data pooled from thousands of children across multiple countries
Step 1: Entry A child under 10 arrives at a health facility with symptoms suggesting TB. Are there danger signs requiring immediate stabilisation?
Step 2: Risk assessment If no danger signs, the clinician assesses risk level. Children under 2, HIV-positive, or with severe acute malnutrition go straight to laboratory testing. Lower-risk children are treated for the most likely non-TB illness and followed up.
Step 3: Lab tests High-risk children and those with persistent symptoms are tested. GeneXpert MTB/RIF on respiratory samples. Urine LF-LAM for HIV-positive children. A positive result triggers treatment immediately.
Step 4: TB detected? If the test result is available: was TB detected? YES means treat immediately. If negative or the test was unavailable, the algorithm continues to contact history.
Step 5: Contact history Was there close household TB contact in the last 12 months? If yes, treat immediately. This step catches children whose test was negative but who have known exposure.
Step 6: The scoring step · the only difference If no confirmed contact: score each symptom. Algorithm A adds chest X-ray findings with additional points. Algorithm B (for clinics without X-ray) assigns higher weights to clinical symptoms to compensate. Both treat if total > 10.
Step 7: Treatment A score above 10 triggers treatment, the same threshold for both algorithms.

Frequently Asked Questions

An MSF nurse palpates 7-year old Vedant's lymph nodes in Mumbai, India. His sister has drug-resistant TB. © Prem Hessenkamp.

How accurate are the flowcharts, and is there risk of over treatment?

False Positives > False Negatives — a deliberate design choice.

Sensitivity · 84–91%

Correctly identifies most children who do have TB — so fewer sick children are missed and go untreated.

Specificity · 3–50%

Some children who don't have TB will still be flagged — a deliberate tradeoff. Missing a case in a disease this deadly costs more than over-treating.

Aren't lab tests more reliable than algorithms?

Not in children. Bacteriological tests often fail to detect pediatric TB. TDAs use clinical, radiographic, and contextual evidence to reflect how diagnosis actually happens in practice.

Are health systems equipped to use TDAs?

Yes. TDAs are designed for low-resource, primary care settings and reduce reliance on laboratory infrastructure.

Is there enough evidence to support TDAs?

Yes. Endorsed by the World Health Organization in 2022, TDAs are backed by multi-country evidence showing strong diagnostic performance and real-world impact.

Is implementation too complex?

The main barrier is not complexity, but policy inertia and incentive structures that favor lab-confirmed cases over clinically diagnosed children.
